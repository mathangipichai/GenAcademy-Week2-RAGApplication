# Texas State Tenant Laws (Texas Property Code Chapter 92)

## 1. Landlord's Duty to Repair or Remedy
Under Texas Property Code Section 92.056, a landlord has a duty to repair or remedy conditions that materially affect the physical health or safety of an ordinary tenant.
- **Conditions covered**: Mold, lack of hot water (must be at least 120°F), sewage backups, pest infestations, broken locks, and structural issues.
- **Notice requirement**: The tenant must be current on rent and must give the landlord written notice of the condition.
- **Method of notice**: If sent by certified mail, return receipt requested, or registered mail, only one notice is required. If sent by regular mail, email, or hand delivery, the tenant must give a second notice if the landlord fails to repair within a reasonable time.
- **Reasonable time**: Texas law presumes 7 days is a reasonable time to make repairs, though this can vary depending on the severity of the condition and availability of parts.
- **Tenant remedies**: If the landlord fails to repair, the tenant may:
  1. Terminate the lease.
  2. Repair and deduct (under strict guidelines in Section 92.0561, up to the cost of one month's rent or $500, whichever is greater).
  3. File a lawsuit in justice court (under Section 92.0563) to obtain an order directing repairs, a reduction in rent, and damages.

## 2. Security Deposits (Chapter 92, Subchapter C)
- **Refund timeline**: Under Section 92.103, a landlord must refund the security deposit or provide a written description and itemized list of deductions on or before the 30th day after the tenant moves out.
- **Condition for refund**: The tenant must provide the landlord with a written forwarding address.
- **Allowable deductions**: Landlords can deduct for damages exceeding normal wear and tear. Deductions cannot be made for normal wear and tear (e.g., worn carpet, peeling paint due to age).
- **Bad faith retention**: If a landlord fails to return the deposit or provide an itemized deduction list within 30 days, they are presumed to have acted in bad faith. Under Section 92.109, a tenant can sue for $100 plus three times the amount of the deposit wrongfully withheld, plus attorney's fees.

## 3. Security Devices (Chapter 92, Subchapter D)
Texas law requires landlords to install specific security devices at the landlord's expense without the tenant needing to request them:
- Keyless bolting devices (deadbolts) on all exterior doors.
- Door viewers (peepholes) on all exterior doors.
- Pin locks and security bars or latch locks on all sliding glass doors.
- Window latches on all windows.
If a landlord fails to install these devices, the tenant can give a written notice requesting installation. If the landlord fails to comply within 3 days, the tenant may terminate the lease or file a lawsuit.

## 4. Retaliation (Chapter 92, Subchapter H)
Under Section 92.331, a landlord is prohibited from retaliating against a tenant who in good faith:
- Requests repairs.
- Complains to a government entity or utility about a habitability issue.
- Establishes or participates in a tenant organization.
Retaliatory actions include eviction, rent increases, reduction in services, or termination of the lease within 6 months of the tenant's action. If the landlord retaliates, the tenant can sue for civil penalties of one month's rent plus $500, court costs, and attorney's fees.
